Founder Launch Plan: Legacy Defenders

A lean, realistic launch plan for getting Legacy Defenders started in Allegheny County with the least friction: first offer, founder roles, cash compensation, deferred legal work, and first-project budgets.

$125 PA LLC filing fee
1 Finished customer before heavy legal spend
0-30 Days to first paid sprint

Purpose

Legacy Defenders should launch as a practical service, not as a complicated company on day one.

The first version of the business has one job: help an executor or heir get control of an inherited home, the belongings inside it, the urgent property issues, and the next financial decision. The company does not need a warehouse, a full crew, custom software, employee payroll, a financing product, or a complex multi-company structure to prove that.

The cheapest responsible path is:

  1. File the minimum business setup needed to invoice and separate money.
  2. Use the existing website, free guide, pricing examples, and care coordinator visit to create the first lead.
  3. Sell one tightly scoped paid project that can be finished in 7-14 days.
  4. Pay present-day work from that project.
  5. Document every hour, cost, decision, photo, mistake, and margin.
  6. Use the first finished customer and profit to fund the legal work that should not be guessed at.

This page is public for transparency, but it is a planning document. It is not legal, tax, insurance, employment, lending, or probate advice. Before Legacy Defenders uses customer contracts, founder equity, referral arrangements, financing, storage custody, or affiliated-company arrangements at scale, those details need attorney, CPA, and insurance review.

Lean launch mission

Finish one paid customer before building the expensive version

The first milestone is not a franchise model. It is one completed customer, a useful case study, clean project records, paid founder work, and enough real numbers to decide what legal and operating structure is worth paying for next.

Launch Rule

Start with the simplest structure that can safely take payment and finish work. Do not spend scarce cash proving theories.

Do Now

Spend now only where it reduces real risk

  • PA entity filing, EIN, separate bank account, basic bookkeeping, and payment processing.
  • General liability and any required vendor insurance certificates before going inside homes.
  • A simple written customer scope with price, payment schedule, exclusions, photo permission, item handling rules, and change-order language.
  • Written contractor/vendor scopes when anyone outside the founders is paid.
  • One-page founder work memo that says who is doing what, how cash work is paid, and that no equity is final until a written agreement is signed.

Defer until proof and cash exist

  • Custom equity documents and multi-entity restructuring.
  • Warehouse leases, trucks, employees, uniforms, and heavy equipment.
  • Estate lending, payment-plan products, or Legacy fronting project cash.
  • Referral-fee programs with attorneys, CPAs, realtors, lenders, funeral homes, or vendors.
  • Franchise, licensing, trademark expansion, and regional operating manuals.

The company should be honest about what it is at launch: a coordinated founder-led service that uses checklists, documentation, QR/photo records, trusted vendors, and practical project management to reduce estate-property overwhelm.

Allegheny County Starting Market

Allegheny County is large enough to support a county-first launch. The numbers below are official or government-source figures where available; the opportunity estimate is a planning assumption.

Metric Current Data Why It Matters
Population About 1.23 million residents, 2025 Census estimate Large local base without needing regional expansion first
People age 65+ 21.2% of residents, 2024 estimate Strong signal for future estate-transition demand
Housing units About 614,000 units, 2025 Census estimate Deep housing base for inherited-home and vacant-home work
Households About 546,000, 2020-2024 Census estimate Large direct-mail, SEO, and referral universe
Owner-occupied housing rate 65.2%, 2020-2024 Census estimate Many estate transitions will involve owned homes
Median owner-occupied home value $227,600, 2020-2024 Census estimate Enough value for many modest preservation projects
All-cause deaths 71,995 from 2020-2024 Roughly 14,400 deaths per year before filtering

Not every death creates a project. Some people rent, some homes transfer smoothly, some heirs already have help, and some properties do not have enough value to justify paid coordination.

A conservative launch assumption is that 1%-2% of annual deaths could become high-fit inherited-home opportunities in Allegheny County. Using roughly 14,400 deaths per year, that implies about 145-290 possible high-fit projects per year before competition, timing, awareness, and referral access. Legacy Defenders only needs the first 6-12 good projects to prove the model.

High-fit early customers usually have:

  • one clear executor or authorized decision maker
  • a safe property in or near Allegheny County
  • belongings that need sorting, routing, donation, sale, storage, or disposal
  • light repair, cleaning, or market-prep needs
  • enough estate value, family budget, or asset-sale potential to pay for the work
  • urgency, distance, grief, or complexity that makes DIY slow and costly

What Waits Until After Customer 1

After one finished customer, Legacy Defenders should have a better legal conversation because it will have facts instead of guesses.

Use the first project profit to fund review of:

  • founder operating agreement and vesting terms
  • IP ownership for website, checklists, QR workflow, pricing sheets, photos, templates, and training materials
  • contractor/subcontractor agreement and worker classification
  • customer agreement and change-order language
  • insurance coverage, storage responsibility, item custody, and limitation-of-liability language
  • affiliated-company disclosures if Rachel, Mike, Keith, or related companies are paid by the project
  • attorney/CPA/realtor/lender/vendor referral rules
  • estate asset-sale rules, consignment-like work, and recordkeeping
  • financing or lender packet only after demand and project controls are proven

The right sequence is:

  1. Get one customer done.
  2. Run the numbers.
  3. Decide what the business actually is.
  4. Pay for documents that match reality.

First Offer

The first offer should be a free care coordinator visit followed by a paid, tightly scoped executor relief sprint.

The free visit is not a vague sales call. It is a useful working session where the family gets a first plan they can keep. Legacy Defenders uses the visit to decide whether the project is a fit and to scope a paid sprint.

Free Care Coordinator Visit

The visit should cover:

  • who has authority to make decisions
  • whether there is active family conflict
  • house access, utilities, safety, pets, mold, water, pests, smoke, or structural concerns
  • urgent deadlines: insurance, taxes, listing, sale, move-out, storage, or vacancy
  • belongings categories and obvious high-value or sensitive items
  • what the family can do themselves
  • what Legacy Defenders could do faster
  • the likely first paid scope and budget range

Paid Executor Relief Sprint

The paid sprint should sound like this:

"We will document the house, help the executor make belongings decisions, tag and route priority items, coordinate the first cleanout/reset, identify urgent repair risks, and deliver a written plan with photos, item notes, budget, and next steps."

This is easier to sell than a full renovation because it solves the immediate problem. It is easier to deliver because it does not require Legacy to promise a complete transformation before the team has proven its process.

Early projects should avoid:

  • no clear decision maker
  • active family disputes that require legal resolution
  • unsafe properties
  • major renovations
  • unfunded jobs where Legacy must front money
  • financing-heavy deals
  • hidden referral economics
  • valuable personal property disputes

Launch Phases

Phase 0: 48 hours

Make the public path coherent: footer link, free guide, first offer, contact flow, and a simple "what happens next" script.

Phase 1: 7 days

Finish the minimum launch kit: intake, checklist, price range, first proposal, budget tracker, payment method, insurance quote, and vendor list.

Phase 2: 0-30 days

Get 10 real conversations, 3 free care visits, and 1 paid sprint proposal. Do founder-led work, not expensive infrastructure.

Phase 3: 30-60 days

Complete the first paid sprint, collect proof, pay cash work, hold reserve, and review every estimate against actuals.

Phase 4: After customer 1

Use project proof and cash to pay for the legal, insurance, and accounting structure that matches the real model.

Phase 5: Customers 2-3

Repeat the sprint or step up to a market-ready reset only after scope control, customer communication, and margin are proven.

Decision Gates

Gate 1

Before free visits

Have a visit checklist, authority questions, safety red flags, and a clear explanation that Legacy does not provide legal or tax advice.

Gate 2

Before taking payment

Have the business/payment path, written scope, deposit terms, insurance plan, photo/item rules, exclusions, and change-order process.

Gate 3

Before hiring labor

Collect enough customer money to cover vendors. Use scoped vendors or contractors before employees unless project volume repeats.

Gate 4

Before larger projects

Finish one sprint with real margin data before accepting $25K+ market-ready work. Finish two clean jobs before $50K+ recovery work.

Gate 5

Before referral programs

Use introductions and education first. Any referral fee, co-marketing, preferred-provider discount, or affiliated relationship needs review.

Gate 6

Before financing

Do not lend Legacy money or promise funding. Financing, asset sales, sale-proceeds repayment, and lender packets wait for attorney/lender review.

Cash Compensation Now

Cash compensation and long-term equity are separate. Equity should not be used to avoid paying people for work the project needs today.

Early cash rules:

  • Customer deposits fund project work before anyone spends heavily.
  • Vendor costs, disposal, supplies, and required outside labor get protected first.
  • Founder labor is priced into the project budget.
  • A 10%-15% reserve stays untouched until invoices, refunds, punch-list items, and closeout are complete.
  • Bonuses are paid from known gross profit, not from revenue.
  • Asset-sale proceeds belong to the estate after approved fees and costs.

Equity Later

The first legal mistake to avoid is promising ownership casually. The second mistake is spending heavily on complicated documents before the team knows what it is building.

Lean approach:

  1. Before customer 1, use a simple written founder work memo: current roles, current cash pay, no verbal equity grant, no authority to bind the company alone, and no hidden side deals.
  2. After customer 1, use profit to pay for a real founder agreement.
  3. Make equity vest over contribution and milestones.
  4. Keep founder-owned service companies separate from the Legacy Defenders platform unless counsel says otherwise.

Equity should consider:

  • platform creation: brand, website, CRM, pricing, reporting, QR workflow, financing packet, future licensing model
  • care model creation: family trust, intake, decision sessions, belongings workflow, coordinator training
  • property model creation: repairs, vendors, quality control, crew standards, market-prep playbook
  • ongoing operating responsibility: who keeps doing the work, carrying risk, improving process, and protecting the brand

Possible vesting milestones for discussion:

Milestone Why It Matters
Signed founder agreement Stops verbal promises from becoming a future fight
1 completed customer Creates real data and funds legal review
3 completed projects Shows repeatability beyond one lucky case
12 completed projects Shows pricing, vendors, and customer communication can repeat
50-60 projects/year Justifies expansion, managers, licensing, or franchise discussion

If a founder stops contributing, leaves, or cannot perform the operating role, unvested equity should return to the company under written terms. Buyback, noncompete/non-solicit limits, IP ownership, deadlock rules, and authority to sign contracts should be decided with counsel.

Project 3: Heavy Recovery Later

A $35,000-$60,000 recovery project may become real later. It is not the cheapest path to launch.

Do not accept heavy recovery until:

  • one executor sprint is finished
  • one market-ready reset is finished or tightly scoped
  • customer authority is clear
  • deposit/draw schedule protects cash
  • insurance and licensed-trade rules are clear
  • change-order rules are written
  • no one is asking Legacy to front major cash

Operating Rules

  • Every project has one authorized decision maker.
  • Every customer gets a written scope before paid work starts.
  • Every paid worker or vendor has a written scope.
  • Every project starts with enough cash collected to avoid floating vendor costs.
  • Every item path is documented: keep, deliver, store, sell, donate, discard, return, or review later.
  • Every photo and item record is handled respectfully and only shared as approved.
  • Legal and tax advice comes from attorneys and CPAs, not Legacy Defenders.
  • Licensed work goes to licensed trades.
  • Storage responsibility, insurance limits, and item custody are written plainly.
  • Referral relationships, discounts, and affiliated-company relationships are disclosed and reviewed before use.
  • Founder pay is a project cost, not imaginary future profit.
  • Equity is written, vested, and separate from current work pay.
  • Every project ends with a postmortem: estimated cost, actual cost, hours, delays, customer issues, margin, and what to price differently next time.
