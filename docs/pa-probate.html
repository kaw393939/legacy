Probate Gives You Authority. It Does Not Give You Time.

In Pennsylvania, the Register of Wills handles probate of wills and grants letters to the personal representative. Those letters give the executor or administrator authority to act for the estate, but they do not magically solve the property problem.

The executor may still have to handle a house full of belongings, mortgage payments, taxes, utilities, insurance, lawn/code issues, repairs, family emotions, and deadlines.

That is where Legacy Defenders fits.

We are not a law firm, tax preparer, lender, realtor, appraiser, or licensed trade contractor. We are the care-led operations team that helps the executor organize the home, belongings, vendors, records, and next decisions while working with the right professionals.

What to do today

Before probate paperwork turns into property damage or a low offer, stabilize the house side.

Start by identifying authority, urgent bills, vacant-home risks, belongings that may contain records or valuables, and whether the estate has equity but not enough cash. We can help organize that picture before you spend larger money.

The PA Probate Timeline Families Often Underestimate

Step 1

Get authority

The will is probated or an administrator is appointed through the Register of Wills. Until authority is clear, major property decisions may be blocked or risky.

Step 2

Notify heirs and beneficiaries

Official county guidance commonly points to notice and certification requirements within three months after letters are granted.

Step 3

Inventory the estate

The executor needs records of estate assets. For a home, that can include the real estate and personal property inside it.

Step 4

File and pay inheritance tax

PA inheritance tax returns are generally due within nine months after death. PA also allows a 5% discount for tax paid within three months.

Step 5

Preserve and distribute value

The home has to be maintained, belongings handled, debts reviewed, records preserved, and sale/keep/rent/move-in decisions made.

Sources and official references:

  • The PA Department of Revenue states inheritance tax rates are 0% for surviving spouse transfers, 4.5% for direct descendants and lineal heirs, 12% for siblings, and 15% for other heirs, with charitable organizations and certain exempt entities excluded. See PA Department of Revenue: Inheritance Tax.
  • PA's REV-1500 form states resident inheritance tax returns must be filed within nine months after death. See PA REV-1500.
  • The PA Department of Revenue says inheritance tax payments are due at death, delinquent after nine months, and eligible for a 5% discount if paid within three months. See Make an Inheritance Tax Payment.
  • Philadelphia's Register of Wills resources include post-probate forms for notice, certification of notice, estate inventory, and status report. See Philadelphia Register of Wills Resources.
  • Chester County's official estate settlement packet describes notice, inventory, inheritance tax, and status-report timing. See Chester County Estate Settlement Packet.

What People Do Not Consider Until It Hurts

Most heirs think probate is paperwork. The house makes it an operating problem.

Mortgage and tax pressure

Payments may continue while the family is still grieving and unsure what to do.

How we help:

We identify the cash-pressure timeline and help prepare a scope for attorney, CPA, lender, or sale-proceeds review.

Lawn, utilities, insurance, and vacant-home risk

Small issues can turn into fines, leaks, theft, pests, frozen pipes, or insurance problems.

How we help:

We create a property-stabilization checklist, photo reports, vendor calls, and urgent task plan.

Belongings block every next step

You cannot paint, repair floors, clean deeply, stage, rent, move in, or sell easily while the house is full.

How we help:

We use QR tags and photos to classify items once, then route them to storage, sale, donation, disposal, delivery, or return.

Family memories make decisions slow

People freeze because throwing something away can feel like losing the person again.

How we help:

Our care coordinator treats belongings as memories first, then helps the executor make practical decisions with dignity.

Estate sale bargain-hunters

Traditional estate sales may bring strangers into the family home to buy personal items at deep discounts.

How we help:

Approved sale items can move to controlled processing/storage where they are cleaned, photographed, listed, tracked, and reviewed.

Contractor chaos

Executors often do not know which repairs matter, who to call, or how to avoid overbuilding.

How we help:

Mike's construction judgment focuses on practical, value-conscious work: clean, safe, modern enough, and ready for the next decision.

Records For PA Inheritance Tax And Estate Accounting

PA's resident inheritance tax return is REV-1500. The executor, attorney, CPA, or tax preparer decides what belongs on that return and how estate expenses, reimbursements, deductions, asset values, donations, and sale proceeds should be handled.

Legacy Defenders helps by keeping the house side organized:

  • utility, mortgage, insurance, tax, lawn, vacant-home, and maintenance notes
  • repair scopes, invoices, receipts, and before/after photos
  • QR inventory exports, item photos, sale logs, storage logs, donation/disposal records, and distribution notes
  • vendor contact records and project status reports
  • financing scopes, draw/milestone notes, and questions for attorney/lender review

The point is not to replace professional advice. The point is to give the professionals a clearer record so the executor is not trying to reconstruct the house project from memory months later.

When There Is Equity But No Cash

Many estates have value trapped in the house, but little available cash to pay the mortgage, utilities, taxes, insurance, lawn care, cleanout, repairs, storage, or market-ready preparation.

Legacy Defenders can help the executor prepare a custom stabilization and funding plan:

  • identify urgent carrying costs and deadlines
  • estimate the work needed to protect the home and unlock value
  • separate must-do stabilization from optional improvements
  • prepare scopes, photos, and budgets for attorney, CPA, family, lender, or sale-proceeds review
  • coordinate payment structures only after the right authority and professional review are in place

The goal is to help the executor and heirs make the best informed decision available: sell, keep, rent, move in, refinance, repair in phases, or use a financing path designed to protect rather than exploit the estate.

Working With Attorneys And CPAs

You can use your own attorney, CPA, or tax preparer. If you do not have them, we can introduce preferred or affiliated professionals who understand probate, inherited homes, estate records, and property coordination.

Because our process is standardized, those professionals may be able to work more efficiently and may offer discounted or packaged rates where available. Their legal or tax advice, engagement terms, and fees come directly from them.

We do not replace the attorney or CPA. We make their job easier by organizing the house side of the problem:

  • property status
  • photos
  • inventory notes
  • color-coded QR item records
  • repair estimates
  • contractor/vendor records
  • storage and sale logs
  • heir distribution notes
  • donation/disposal records
  • financing scope questions
  • executor decision history

The executor and attorney should review authority before major decisions such as borrowing against the property, signing lien documents, selling the home, distributing property, or approving large repairs.
