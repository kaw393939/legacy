Start Here: You Can Do A Lot Yourself

If you inherited a home, you may be able to handle much of the work yourself. Many families can secure the property, gather bills, sort belongings, call vendors, and make a plan without hiring a full service team.

The problem is that most people do not know what they are about to run into. The work is not just "clean out the house." It is a mix of grief, family decisions, property risk, deadlines, carrying costs, repair choices, records, taxes, sale timing, and cash pressure.

This guide helps you see the whole problem before it becomes expensive.

Before anything irreversible

Call before you accept a low offer, pay for cleanout, start repairs, or throw belongings away.

The first conversation does not obligate you to a large project. It can help you avoid the most common early mistakes: missing documents, losing sentimental items, overpaying vendors, underpricing the house, or spending money in the wrong order.

What To Do In The First Week

The first week is about preventing small problems from turning into expensive ones.

1

Confirm authority

Find the will if there is one, identify the executor or likely administrator, and talk with an attorney before making major estate decisions.

2

Secure the property

Check locks, keys, windows, heat, water, electric, alarms, mail, and signs of leaks, pests, break-ins, or weather damage.

3

Gather bills

Mortgage, taxes, insurance, utilities, HOA/condo dues, subscriptions, credit cards, medical bills, funeral costs, and vendor invoices.

4

Pause the cleanout

Do not throw things away too quickly. Documents, photos, titles, military records, passwords, jewelry, cash, and sentimental items are often mixed into ordinary boxes.

5

Photograph first

Take room-by-room photos before moving items. Photos help family decisions, insurance questions, repair planning, and estate records.

6

Make the first budget

Use the worksheet below to estimate what the estate may have to carry, clean, repair, store, sell, transfer, or finance.

Things Families Often Miss

Most surprise costs happen because nobody made a complete list early enough.

Vacant-home risk

Insurance, heat, pipes, lawn, snow, pests, theft, leaks, code notices, and access problems can matter before the family has made a sale decision.

Belongings slow everything

Repairs, cleaning, photos, appraisals, showings, and contractor work are delayed when the house is full and family decisions are unresolved.

Cash pressure builds quietly

Mortgage, taxes, utilities, insurance, cleanout, storage, attorney, filing, and repair costs can arrive before sale proceeds exist.

Records get scattered

Receipts, invoices, donations, disposal costs, repair photos, and family item decisions are hard to reconstruct months later.

Repairs can overgrow

Some houses need only stabilization and clean paint. Others need licensed trades. Without a scope, costs can expand quickly.

Deadlines still move

In Pennsylvania, inheritance tax timing and estate administration tasks continue even while the family is still deciding what to do with the home.

Why Moving Fast Matters

Moving fast does not mean rushing into a bad sale or spending money blindly. It means getting enough information early to stop the estate from drifting.

Days 1-7

Secure the home, gather bills, photograph rooms, identify authority, and note immediate risks.

Weeks 2-4

Sort priority belongings, estimate costs, decide what professionals are needed, and choose a first path: stabilize, clean, sell as-is, market prep, rent, or keep.

Months 1-3

Finish inventory, route belongings, manage carrying costs, review tax/payment timing, and decide whether repairs or financing make sense.

Months 3-9

Prepare professional records, sale documents, tax information, invoices, and final project decisions before deadlines and carrying costs pile up.

Official Pennsylvania timing note: the PA Department of Revenue says inheritance tax payments are due at death, become delinquent after nine months, and may qualify for a 5% discount if paid within three months. The rates depend on the beneficiary relationship.

Official References

  • Pennsylvania inheritance tax is imposed based on the value transferred and the beneficiary's relationship to the decedent. The PA Department of Revenue lists rates of 0% for surviving spouse transfers, 4.5% for direct descendants and lineal heirs, 12% for siblings, and 15% for other heirs, with certain exemptions. See PA Department of Revenue: Inheritance Tax.
  • Allegheny County's inheritance tax page notes a 5% discount if the tax is paid within three months after death. See Allegheny County: Inheritance Tax.
  • Allegheny County publishes probate and administration fee schedules through the Register of Wills / Orphans' Court resources. See Allegheny County: Probate Fees.
  • Pennsylvania realty transfer tax is 1% at the state level, and Allegheny County notes local realty transfer tax can bring the combined rate to 2%-5% depending on municipality and school district. See PA Department of Revenue: Realty Transfer Tax and Allegheny County: Realty Transfer Taxes.
